## The hierarchy of controls

The hierarchy of controls ranks hazard-control strategies from most to least
effective:

1. **Elimination** — physically remove the hazard.
2. **Substitution** — replace the hazard with a less hazardous option.
3. **Engineering controls** — isolate people from the hazard (e.g. guarding,
   ventilation, enclosures).
4. **Administrative controls** — change the way people work (e.g. procedures,
   training, job rotation, signage).
5. **Personal protective equipment (PPE)** — protect the worker with equipment
   worn on the body.

Elimination and substitution are most effective because they reduce the hazard
at the source and do not depend on worker behavior. PPE is least effective
because it does nothing to reduce the hazard itself and relies entirely on
correct, consistent use.

## Leading vs. lagging indicators

- **Leading indicators** are proactive measures taken before an incident
  occurs (e.g. number of safety audits completed, percentage of corrective
  actions closed on time, training hours delivered).
- **Lagging indicators** measure outcomes after they occur (e.g. recordable
  injury rate, lost-time incident rate, days away from work).

A mature safety program tracks both, but emphasizes leading indicators because
they create opportunities to intervene before harm occurs.

## Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)

The PDCA cycle is a continuous-improvement framework used in safety management
systems:

- **Plan** — establish objectives and the processes needed to deliver results.
- **Do** — implement the plan.
- **Check** — monitor and measure results against objectives.
- **Act** — take action to continually improve performance.
